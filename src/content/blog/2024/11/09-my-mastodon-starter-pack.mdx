## Introduction

Sometimes, I invite someone to join [Mastodon](https://joinmastodon.org/), and I end up sharing the same links and information repeatedly.

Following the [“manual until it hurts”](https://indieweb.org/manual_until_it_hurts) [IndieWeb](https://indieweb.org/) practice, after repeating the same things a few times, it is time to “automate” it. Well, in this case, just put everything into one blog post, I guess.

If you’re looking for whom to follow — I don’t have a “useful” single-click deceptive pattern that will bulk add hundreds of accounts, making those who are already rich richer, and creating an illusion of activity with numbers going up. But I have a [“Whom to Follow?”](#whom-to-follow) section later in the post, with a general recommendation of how to find people you’d want to follow yourself.
</aside>

## Why join Mastodon?

I’ve been using Mastodon actively for more than two years now: on November 6th, it was my second “fediversary” (last year, I wrote a [“My First ‘Fediversary’”](/first-fediversary/) post). In that post, I described why _I_ joined. I’m pretty happy there!

If I did send you a link to this post, that means I would be happy if you would join. I am not on any other social network, and I would love to stay connected with you.

That _**is**_ the main reason.

<aside>
	<details>
		<summary>Why not other social networks? </summary>

In short: they’re not decentralized. Even if their _protocol_ is (at least in theory), there is usually a single entity that controls everything, backed by venture capital of questionable origins. I’ve been on the Web for more than 20 years, and I’m tired of services changing owners and going away.

If a single instance goes away, you can always move to another. If something happens with Mastodon itself, the Fediverse is still there.

There is a critical mass of people who care, and while moving servers can sometimes be painful, it _is_ possible, and you can continue following everyone you did before.

	</details>
</aside>

<aside>
	<details>
		<summary>Why not to choose the “default” instance?</summary>

If you _really_ don’t want to think, and you don’t have any friends, whose Mastodon accounts you know, you can join the [mastodon.social](https://mastodon.social/about) instance.

However, I strongly recommend you find a smaller home. You will still be able to follow people from other instances — that’s the main point of decentralization — and you’ll have a smaller and neater neighbourhood, with better moderation and people who will more likely share your interests closer to you.

I did also omit some other larger instances like [hachyderm.io](https://hachyderm.io/about) and [fosstodon.org](https://fosstodon.org/about), as, in my opinion, they are already _too big_.

## Whom to Follow?

First, look at the [front-end.social’s “directory”](https://front-end.social/directory?order=active&local=true): it shows everyone who opted-in to be present there. If you had followed people from the front-end community before, you’ll likely see some familiar faces. And if front-end.social won’t be enough for you: look at the directories for other instances I posted above, as well as at the [fedidevs](https://fedidevs.com/) directory that I already mentioned.

And — always a good way to find people — go to the “following” tab on anyone’s profile, and, if they did not hide it, you could also find someone to follow there. For example, here is [my “following” list](https://front-end.social/@kizu/following).

<aside>
There is also another good way to find people: search for “Mastodon” in the search of your old and rotten social network, choose “People you follow” and then look at the “Latest” and “People” tabs. They will likely contain mentions of where people are moving to, either in posts, or in usernames and profiles.

I am not going to link to anywhere directly from here, but maybe this will help you: `/search?q=mastodon&f=live&pf=on`
</aside>


## Which Mastodon Client to Use?

By default, the official web-app might not be ideal. As soon as you find its UX confusing, I highly recommend checking out one of many other [third-party apps](https://joinmastodon.org/apps#:~:text=Browse-,third%2Dparty%20apps,-All), in particular:

1. [Phanpy](https://phanpy.social/) by [Lim Chee Aun](https://cheeaun.com/) ([@cheeaun@mastodon.social](https://mastodon.social/@cheeaun)) — “A minimalistic opinionated Mastodon web client”. This is the main app I’m using both on the desktop, and the mobile. It is great! So many innovative features! The best part? It is just a _static_ (!) web app — you can fork it, and then use it either just from localhost, or host it yourself on any of the free services — GitHub pages, Netlify, or anything else. See, for example, [my fork](https://phanpy.kizu.dev/) — I added some CSS overrides just for myself.

2. [Ivory](https://tapbots.com/ivory/) by [TabBots](https://tapbots.com/) — an iOS app from the creators of “Tweetbot”. I am not using it, but I have warm memories of using Tweetbot back in the day, and if this is the experience you’re missing as well — you can try it! (but it is paid and has a subscription model).

3. [Elk](https://elk.zone/) — I did not try it, but I heard many good things about it. It might look very familiar to you, and if this familiarity is what you’re craving — you can try it!


## What is the Basic Etiquette?

I have no idea! Various instances have differences in how people use Mastodon. Before choosing an instance, I recommend carefully reading out their Code of Conduct and other rules they might have.

If I were to briefly mention something, it would be this:

1. Fill up your profile: userpic, name, links, etc.

2. Write an `#introduction` post. You can search for it in Mastodon to see how others do it, for example: https://front-end.social/tags/introduction

3. Start following people! Now, people could recognize you, or just understand who followed them, and not look at a newly created account with the default avatar, not knowing what to expect.

4. Start using Mastodon! Read what people write, and post your own stuff.

5. I recommend not to post links and advertise yourself _right away_. In general, self-promotion is welcome on most servers, but it is better to first acclimatize a bit to how things work, introduce yourself, show that you’re an actual person, and only then plug your work.

6. Similarly, I don’t recommend jumping into replies to everyone, especially if you do not agree with the other person, or have “not a question, but a comment”. Read first, think second, and only then decide if you can contribute to the conversation tactfully and responsibly. You know, be a decent, empathic person.

7. When posting something, do it accessibly: add proper alternative descriptions to images. I recommend [“My Approach to Alt Text”](https://adrianroselli.com/2024/05/my-approach-to-alt-text.html) by [Adrian Roselli](https://adrianroselli.com/) ([@aardrian@toot.cafe](https://toot.cafe/@aardrian)), and do not use “fancy characters” and unusual markup, as it can be frustrating to get read to you if you’re using a screen reader.

This is not an extensive list. Think with your own head, follow the Code of Conducts, and be a good human.
